## bottles / wineskins (Matthew 9:17 etc.)
- **"bottle(s)" — PERIOD.** Attested KJV 25x, Geneva1599 22x (spelled "bottel(s)"), Wycliffe 20x (spelled "botel(is)"), Tyndale 3x. Continuous use 1382→1611.
- **"wineskin(s)" — ANACHRONISM.** Zero hits in all four databases (`LIKE '%wineskin%'`). Not period vocabulary at all; it's a later (post-KJV) translational coinage used by ERV/NASB-family versions.
- Note: the "bottles were expensive glass, reserved for kings" argument in the doc is a category error — biblical "bottles" denotes a **skin/leather vessel**, not glass; "bottle" in EModE covered any liquid container regardless of material.

## couch / crouch (Job 38:40 etc.)
- **"couch" — PERIOD**, robustly. Attested KJV 14x, Geneva 1599 (Job 7:13, Ps 6:6), and in Chaucer's Canterbury Tales (couche/couched/coucheth, 14th c.), and in King James's own 1585 Essayes ("couch", "coucht", "coucheth" — lines ~10556, 18285, 19213).
- The doc's own "14th century" first-use claim, if true, would make it ~250 years old by 1611 — i.e. thoroughly naturalized, not evidence against period use. The doc's reasoning here inverts itself (an early attestation date is used as if it disqualifies rather than confirms).
- "Crouch" (NASB's word) is a *different, later* verb; not what any witness translation uses at Job 38:40.

## divers / diverse ("diver" 1511 claim, Mark 13:18)
- **"divers/diverse" — PERIOD**, extremely well attested: KJV 45x, Geneva1599 44x (spelled "diuers"), Tyndale 12x, Wycliffe 10x (spelled "diuers/diuerse"), plus Chaucer (30+ hits) and King James's own Essayes (2 hits). Continuous attestation 1382→1611, i.e. it predates the cited "1511" date by ~130 years.
- **Document error**: the cited Merriam-Webster link is for "diver" (one who dives/a swimmer, from the verb "dive"), a *different word* from "divers/diverse" (Old French/Latin *diversus*, "various, several"). Citing "diver" as if it evidenced "divers" is an etymology mix-up.
- **Citation error**: the quoted verse "For nation shall rise against nation... earthquakes in divers places..." is **Mark 13:8**, not Mark 13:18. KJV.db Mark 13:18 actually reads "And pray ye that your flight be not in the winter." Verified directly.

## tables / tablets (Exodus 34:1)
- **"tables" (of stone) — PERIOD, and the universal period rendering.** KJV, Geneva1599 ("Tables of stone"), and Wycliffe ("tablis") all use the tables/tablis word family at Exodus 34:1; no witness uses "tablet(s)." KJV.db also has 3 hits for "tablet" elsewhere (different sense/context), so "tablet" existed as a word but was not the translators' choice for the stone tables.
- The claim that "tables" should become "tablets" is **not supported** — it would actually break consistency with every period witness.

## matrix / womb (Exodus 34:19 etc.)
- **"matrix" — PERIOD, attested directly in Geneva 1599** at Numbers 18:15: "All that first openeth **the matrice** of any flesh..." (spelling variant of matrix). This is a pre-1611 English Bible translation using the word.
- Even taking the doc's own claimed first-use date (1555) at face value, that is 56 years before 1611 — again the doc's reasoning inverts an early-attestation data point into a disqualifier.
- Not found in the Middle English corpora on hand (Canterbury Tales, Quinte Essence, ME Reader) — consistent with a 16th-century (not medieval) loanword, but that's irrelevant to whether it could appear in 1611.
- Geneva/Wycliffe elsewhere prefer "wombe" for the same Hebrew phrase — so KJV's "matrix" is a minority but genuine period word choice, not an intrusion.

## charity / love (1 Corinthians 13)
- **"charity" — PERIOD, older than "love" in this specific textual tradition.** Wycliffe (1382) already reads "charite" at 1 Cor 13:1,4,13 (from Vulgate *caritas*), 200+ years before KJV. King James's own 1585 Essayes uses "charity/Charites" 10 times.
- Geneva (1599) and Tyndale (1526) both chose "love" instead at 1 Cor 13 — so within the English Bible tradition there were *two* competing period choices (charity via Wycliffe/Vulgate lineage; love via Tyndale/Geneva), and KJV picked "charity" for Paul's agape passages specifically.
- KJV.db has "charity" 24x and "love" 546x — charity was never a *replacement* for love throughout the KJV; both words coexist, "charity" reserved mostly for the Pauline sense of active goodwill/self-giving.

## spirit of error (1 John 4:6)
- **"spirit of error" — PERIOD, stable across 3 centuries.** Geneva1599: "spirit of errour"; Wycliffe: "spirit of errour"; KJV: "spirit of error." Identical phrase, unbroken from 1382 to 1611. It is the deliberate antithesis of "spirit of truth" in the same verse (a test-the-spirits idiom, cf. 1 John 4:1) — theologically coherent, not nonsensical.

## destroyed / perish (Hosea 4:6)
- **"destroyed" — PERIOD, word-for-word identical to Geneva 1599**: "My people are destroyed for lacke of knowledge..." No Tyndale witness exists for Hosea (Tyndale's OT work did not extend to the Minor Prophets). Wycliffe has a structurally different verb ("was stille," from a different Vulgate reading) so it isn't a same-word comparison there.
- Claim that "destroyed is a new word in the KJV" is **not supported** — it's inherited verbatim from Geneva 1599.
- "Perish" is also genuinely period (KJV 153x) but there's no positive evidence it was the original reading at Hosea 4:6; Geneva's agreement with KJV argues the other way.

## heaven / heavens (Genesis 1:1)
- KJV, Geneva1599, Tyndale, and Wycliffe **all four** render Gen 1:1 with singular "heaven," not plural. No period witness supports the "heavens" plural memory at this verse.
- BUT: KJV itself uses plural "the heavens and the earth" two verses later, in the *same* creation narrative (Gen 2:1, 2:4) — so plural rendering of the same Hebrew phrase is not foreign to the KJV's own usage, just not what it chose at 1:1. KJV overall has "heavens" 127x elsewhere. Frame this as a translational-choice variation, not a corruption per se, when this reaches Phase 6.
- No comma after "In the beginning" is the KJV's consistent style (cf. John 1:1 "In the beginning was the Word..." — also no comma), so the "missing comma" is not anomalous punctuation.

## lion and the lamb (Isaiah 11:6, 65:25)
- Confirmed: **no KJV verse anywhere pairs "lion" lying/dwelling with "lamb."** Only co-occurrences of lion+lamb in the whole KJV: (1) 1 Sam 17:34, lion attacking/eating a lamb (opposite sense); (2) Isa 11:6, "wolf...with the lamb" / "calf and...young lion and fatling" (lion paired with calf, not lamb); (3) Isa 65:25, "wolf and lamb," lion separately eats straw. Same pattern in Geneva1599 and Wycliffe.
- This is a genuine, well-attested folk conflation of two adjacent clauses (wolf/lamb + lion/calf), not a textual corruption — worth flagging as a case where "memory" evidence should probably NOT drive a restoration, since no witness ever supported it.

## eyes to see, ears to hear (parable-preface claim)
- "eyes to see...ears to hear" together occurs only twice in the whole KJV: Deut 29:4 and Ezekiel 12:2 — never as a parable preface.
- The actual recurring Gospel formula ("He that hath ears to hear, let him hear," 10 hits) never includes an "eyes" clause and always comes AFTER the parable, not before. The doc's claimed remembered wording ("For those with eyes to see...before beginning a parable") doesn't match the position or content of any attested formula in any witness.

## crush / bruise (Genesis 3:15, "crushing the serpent's head")
- KJV/Geneva both read "bruise...bruise" (symmetrical head/heel verb); Tyndale reads "tread...tread"; Wycliffe reads "breke" (head) / "sette aspies to" (heel, i.e. lie in wait for — an asymmetrical Vulgate-derived reading).
- "Crush" is genuine KJV vocabulary (11 hits elsewhere, e.g. Numbers 22:25 "crushed Balaam's foot") — so the word was available to the translators; its absence here looks like a deliberate word choice (preserving head/heel symmetry with one verb), not an accidental drop.

## Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9-13)
- The doc's "Current KJV" block does NOT match KJV.db. Actual KJV.db reads "thy name...Thy kingdom...our debts, as we forgive our debtors" throughout — i.e., it already matches most of what the doc labels "Memory," not what it labels "Current KJV." The "Current KJV" text quoted ("your name/your kingdom/us...we also have forgiven our debtors") is a modern formal-equivalence version's wording (reads like ESV/NRSV), mislabeled.
- The doxology "For thine is the kingdom, and the power, and the glory, for ever. Amen." **is already present** in KJV.db Matthew 6:13, verbatim identical to Geneva1599 and near-identical to Tyndale. It is NOT missing. Only Wycliffe (Vulgate-based) lacks it — a known, well-documented Greek-manuscript-tradition textual variant (Byzantine has it, Alexandrian/Vulgate mostly doesn't), not evidence of 1611 corruption.
- "Trespasses" IS period and IS in the KJV — but at Matt 6:14-15 ("if ye forgive men their trespasses"), not v.12 (which reads "debts...debtors" in KJV/Geneva/Wycliffe; Tyndale has "treaspases/treaspacers" at v.12). The popular memory of "trespasses" at v.12 likely comes from the Anglican Book of Common Prayer liturgical text, not the KJV Bible text itself. "Trespassers" (the doc's noun form for the second half) is not attested in any witness checked — Tyndale/Wycliffe phrase it as "them that trespass against us," not as the noun "trespassers."
