**Owner rule (2026-07-15), binding on all authenticity work:**

1. **The King James Bible (KJV, KJV.db, and its lineage copies KJVPCE/AKJV/RNKJV/UKJV/Webster) can NEVER be used as a reference for period authenticity.** The KJV *is* the corrupted text under audit — "the word appears in the KJV" is circular evidence and proves nothing about 1611 English. Reasoning like "verified against the actual KJV.db verse text, therefore period" (used in the 2026-07-14 second-opinion pass) is exactly the forbidden move; that pass's conclusions were overruled by the owner for this reason.

2. **The KJV MAY be used for finding similar words**: locating analogous/parallel words and renderings for a rare word — e.g., "what word does the KJV use elsewhere for this same Strong's number?" — is allowed and useful when advising an alternate. Using the KJV as a phrasing/analogy source is fine; using it as an authenticity witness is not.

**Why:** Premise Revision (Decision Log #5) — all written texts are altered-timeline products and merely advisory, and the KJV specifically is the *target* of the alteration. Authenticity judgments must rest on the independent pre-1611 corpora (Wycliffe, Tyndale, Geneva, Middle English texts, the Essayes — themselves advisory but at least not the audited document) and on knowledge of Early Modern English generally.

**How to apply:** when judging whether a word is period, never cite KJV occurrence or a KJV verse as support. If the only ground for "period" would be its presence in the KJV, the verdict is at best unproven — lean `suspect` and advise an alternate found via the KJV's parallel renderings (allowed use) or the period corpora. See also [[corpus-quirks]] for spelling-variant search pitfalls in the independent corpora.
