# Rare word witness batch 0001 — proposals (entries 1–40)

## accuser → adversary — Revelation of John 12:10
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Per the 2026-07-16 owner ruling, merely re-inflecting the flagged word ("accuser → accusers" or reverting to "accuser") is invalid — a genuinely different word is required, kept singular to preserve subject–verb agreement with "is cast down." "Adversary" is the KJV's own standing epithet for Satan in this same accusing role (1 Peter 5:8, "your adversary the devil... as a roaring lion, walketh about, seeking whom he may devour"), so it is period-authentic and theologically apt; the following "which accused them" still reads naturally with "adversary" as its antecedent.
- NEW: And I heard a loud voice saying in heaven, Now is come salvation, and strength, and the kingdom of our God, and the power of his Christ: for the adversary of our brethren is cast down, which accused them before our God day and night.

## accustomed → have learned — Jeremiah 13:23
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Owner ruling 2026-07-16 — "learned" here is a past participle, so the auxiliary must change with it: "are accustomed" → "have learned." Wycliffe ("han lerned yuel") and DRC ("have learned evil") both attest the *have* construction.
- NEW: Can the Ethiopian change his skin, or the leopard his spots? then may ye also do good, that have learned to do evil.

## acknowledgement → knowledge — Colossians 2:2
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: DRC's "unto the knowledge of the mystery of God" directly attests "knowledge" as the sense, and Geneva/ASV's "to know the mystery" confirms the meaning; the substitution is sound and grammatical.
- NEW: That their hearts might be comforted, being knit together in love, and unto all riches of the full assurance of understanding, to the knowledge of the mystery of God, and of the Father, and of Christ;

## acquainted → knowest — Psalms 139:3
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "art perceived with all my ways" reverses the agency — it is God who knows the psalmist's ways, not the reverse — and "perceived with" is not idiomatic. BBE's "have knowledge of all my ways" confirms the sense of intimate knowing; "knowest," a common KJV verb form already used earlier in the same verse's construction pattern ("Thou compassest... and knowest"), restores both grammar and meaning.
- NEW: Thou compassest my path and my lying down, and knowest all my ways.

## acquainted → familiar with — Isaiah 53:3
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Perceived with grief" is not idiomatic English in any register. Geneva's "hath experience of infirmities" and the general sense (one who has known grief intimately) are best carried by "familiar with," a common word that keeps the same preposition pattern as the original "acquainted with."
- NEW: He is despised and rejected of men; a man of sorrows, and familiar with grief: and we hid as it were our faces from him; he was despised, and we esteemed him not.

## acquainting → guiding — Ecclesiastes 2:3
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Driving mine heart with wisdom" is obscure and ill-fitting; ASV, BBE, ACV, and BSB all render this "guiding," a common word that clearly conveys wisdom's governance over the seeker's pursuit of pleasure.
- NEW: I sought in mine heart to give myself unto wine, yet guiding mine heart with wisdom; and to lay hold on folly, till I might see what was that good for the sons of men, which they should do under the heaven all the days of their life.

## acquit → clear — Job 10:14
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Thou wilt not innocent me" misuses "innocent" as a verb, which is not standard usage even in Early Modern English. Geneva's "wilt not holde mee giltlesse" and BBE's "would not make me clear from sin" support "clear," a common verb (used elsewhere in the KJV corpus, including the parallel Nahum 1:3 entry below) that fits the grammar exactly.
- NEW: If I sin, then thou markest me, and thou wilt not clear me from mine iniquity.

## acquit → clear — Nahum 1:3
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Darby and ASV both render this "will/doth not at all clear [the guilty]," an exact structural match; "clear" is common, grammatical, and requires no other change to the verse.
- NEW: The Lord is slow to anger, and great in power, and will not at all clear the wicked: the Lord hath his way in the whirlwind and in the storm, and the clouds are the dust of his feet.

## acre → rood — I Samuel 14:14
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Per the 2026-07-16 owner ruling, "acre" is common in modern English but rare within the KJV corpus itself, so it must go rather than merely fix CUR's broken "an half furrows of land." "Rood" is a genuine period English land measure (a quarter-acre, in ordinary agricultural use in Early Modern England) naming the same kind of small parcel a yoke of oxen might plow, and it slots into the identical "half a ___ of land" frame without further rephrasing.
- NEW: And that first slaughter, which Jonathan and his armourbearer made, was about twenty men, within as it were an half rood of land, which a yoke of oxen might plow.

## acres → roods — Isaiah 5:10
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Per the 2026-07-16 owner ruling, "acres" must also be replaced, not kept, despite being common in modern English. "Roods" (see the parallel I Samuel 14:14 entry above) is the same genuine period land-measure word, distinct from "acre," and drops into the sentence without further rephrasing; CUR's "yokes" is rejected as confusing since a "yoke" properly names a team of oxen, not an area, and would clash with the yoke-of-oxen image already used elsewhere in this batch.
- NEW: Yea, ten roods of vineyard shall yield one bath, and the seed of an homer shall yield an ephah.

## actions → doings — I Samuel 2:3
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Doings" is a common, well-attested KJV word (e.g. "your doings" elsewhere in the corpus) and preserves the sense that all human deeds are weighed by God; grammar is unaffected.
- NEW: Talk no more so exceeding proudly; let not arrogancy come out of your mouth: for the Lord is a God of knowledge, and by him doings are weighed.

## activity → able men — Genesis 47:6
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Men of army" is nonsensical. RWebster and ASV both render this "able men," a clear, common phrase confirmed also by DRC's "industrious men" and BSB's "talented men."
- NEW: The land of Egypt is before thee; in the best of the land make thy father and brethren to dwell; in the land of Goshen let them dwell: and if thou knowest any able men among them, then make them rulers over my cattle.

## adamant → diamond — Ezekiel 3:9
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: The word choice is fine (BBE and BSB both render "diamond"), but CUR's article is wrong ("an diamond" before a consonant). Only the article needs correction.
- NEW: As a diamond harder than flint have I made thy forehead: fear them not, neither be dismayed at their looks, though they be a rebellious house.

## adamant → diamond — Zechariah 7:12
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Same article error as Ezekiel 3:9 ("an diamond stone"); the word itself is acceptable (compare BBE's "hardest stone" and BSB's use of "flint" for the same image), but the indefinite article must be "a" before a consonant.
- NEW: Yea, they made their hearts as a diamond stone, lest they should hear the law, and the words which the Lord of hosts hath sent in his spirit by the former prophets: therefore came a great wrath from the Lord of hosts.

## addicted → devoted — I Corinthians 16:15
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Appointed themselves" implies external assignment, but the sense is self-giving wholehearted service. RWebster and Darby both render this "devoted themselves," a common word that captures the meaning of "addicted themselves to the ministry" precisely.
- NEW: I beseech you, brethren, (ye know the house of Stephanas, that it is the firstfruits of Achaia, and that they have devoted themselves to the ministry of the saints,)

## addition → wreath — I Kings 7:30
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "at the side of every certain" is nonsense — "certain" was left as a bare adjective with no noun. ASV and ACV both render this "wreaths... at the side of each," and Darby has "garland"; "wreath" is common and fits the architectural ornament being described.
- NEW: And every base had four brasen wheels, and plates of brass: and the four corners thereof had undersetters: under the laver were undersetters molten, at the side of every wreath.

## additions → ornaments — I Kings 7:29
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Ornaments" is common, grammatical, and matches the general sense confirmed by ASV's "wreaths" and BBE's "steps" for the same decorative feature.
- NEW: And on the borders that were between the ledges were lions, oxen, and cherubims: and upon the ledges there was a base above: and beneath the lions and oxen were certain ornaments made of thin work.

## additions → ornaments — I Kings 7:36
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Ornaments round about" is sound, matching DRC's "added round about" and ASV's "wreaths round about" for the same engraved decoration.
- NEW: For on the plates of the ledges thereof, and on the borders thereof, he graved cherubims, lions, and palm trees, according to the proportion of every one, and ornaments round about.

## administered → ministered — II Corinthians 8:19
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Wycliffe, Geneva, YLT, and Darby all render this "ministered," a common word already frequent in the KJV corpus, with no loss of meaning.
- NEW: And not that only, but who was also chosen of the churches to travel with us with this grace, which is ministered by us to the glory of the same Lord, and declaration of your ready mind:

## administered → ministered — II Corinthians 8:20
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Wycliffe, Geneva, and YLT support "ministered" as an equally valid, more common rendering alongside the witnesses that keep "administered."
- NEW: Avoiding this, that no man should blame us in this abundance which is ministered by us:

## administration → ministration — II Corinthians 9:12
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Geneva, YLT, Darby, and ASV all render this "ministration," an authentic KJV-family word (compare its use in 2 Corinthians 3) that is more familiar than "administration" in this liturgical sense.
- NEW: For the ministration of this service not only supplieth the want of the saints, but is abundant also by many thanksgivings unto God;

## administrations → ministries — I Corinthians 12:5
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "differences of ministers" wrongly shifts the sense from types of service to persons who serve. Wycliffe and DRC both render this "diversities of ministries," which preserves the original meaning (varieties of service, one Lord) with a common word.
- NEW: And there are differences of ministries, but the same Lord.

## admiration → flattering — Jude 1:16
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "having men's persons in marvel" is an awkward, unclear construction. BSB's "they flatter others for their own advantage" captures the sense precisely — showing favoritism toward persons for gain — with a common, unmistakable verb.
- NEW: These are murmurers, complainers, walking after their own lusts; and their mouth speaketh great swelling words, flattering men's persons because of advantage.

## admiration → marvelled greatly — Revelation 17:6
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Owner ruling 2026-07-17 — multi-word change aligning with MEV, ESV, RSV, and RSVCE; the agent's "wondered with great wonder" was duplicative. Spelled "marvelled" per KJV corpus convention (30+ uses; "marveled" has 0) — flip to single-l if modern spelling is preferred.
- NEW: And I saw the woman drunken with the blood of the saints, and with the blood of the martyrs of Jesus: and when I saw her, I marvelled greatly.

## admired → marvelled at — II Thessalonians 1:10
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR drops the required preposition ("to be marvelled in all them" should be "marvelled at"), as shown by YLT, Darby, and ASV, all of which use "wondered/marvelled AT." The single-word swap needs "at" restored for correct grammar.
- NEW: When he shall come to be glorified in his saints, and to be marvelled at in all them that believe (because our testimony among you was believed) in that day.

## ado → tumult — Mark 5:39
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Webster, RWebster, YLT, Darby, and ASV all render this "tumult," a common and well-attested word for the commotion of mourners.
- NEW: And when he was come in, he saith unto them, Why make ye this tumult, and weep? the damsel is not dead, but sleepeth.
