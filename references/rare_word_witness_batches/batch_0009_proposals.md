# Rare word witness batch 0009 — proposals (entries 321–360)

## benches → seats — Ezekiel 27:6
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Seats" is a genuinely different, common word that matches Wycliffe's own rendering ("seetis") of this verse directly.
- NEW: Of the oaks of Bashan have they made thine oars; the company of the Ashurites have made thy seats of ivory, brought out of the isles of Chittim.

## benefactors → bountiful — Luke 22:25
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Geneva1599 attests "are called bountifull" directly; "bountiful" is a genuinely different word from "benefactors," and the same root stands elsewhere in the KJV corpus (Isaiah 32:5, "the churl said to be bountiful"), confirming it as a common, period-authentic term for one styled generous.
- NEW: And he said unto them, The kings of the Gentiles exercise lordship over them; and they that exercise authority upon them are called bountiful.

## benevolence → her due — I Corinthians 7:3
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "due good" is vague and awkward. ASV's and Darby's "render unto the wife her due" attest a genuinely different, common construction, preserved with the parallel clause about the husband unchanged.
- NEW: Let the husband render unto the wife her due: and likewise also the wife unto the husband.

## berries → olives — Isaiah 17:6
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Olives" is a genuinely different, extremely common word that preserves the sense of the small fruit remaining after an olive tree is shaken, consistent with the surrounding imagery.
- NEW: Yet gleaning grapes shall be left in it, as the shaking of an olive tree, two or three olives in the top of the uppermost bough, four or five in the outmost fruitful branches thereof, saith the Lord God of Israel.

## berries → olives — James 3:12
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "olive olives" is a broken duplication. ASV's "yield olives" and Geneva1599's "bring forth oliues" attest dropping the redundant adjective "olive" entirely, leaving the common noun alone.
- NEW: Can the fig tree, my brethren, bear olives? either a vine, figs? so can no fountain both yield salt water and fresh.

## besom → broom — Isaiah 14:23
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "mire of destruction" wrongly substitutes a swampy image for the sweeping image the verse requires. ACV's and BSB's "broom of destruction" attest a genuinely different, common word naming the same sweeping implement.
- NEW: I will also make it a possession for the bittern, and pools of water: and I will sweep it with the broom of destruction, saith the Lord of hosts.

## bestead → distressed — Isaiah 8:21
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "hardly hard" is a broken duplication. Webster's, RWebster's, and ASV's "distressed" all attest a genuinely different, common word for the same affliction, and fits the "hardly ___ and hungry" frame directly.
- NEW: And they shall pass through it, hardly distressed and hungry: and it shall come to pass, that when they shall be hungry, they shall fret themselves, and curse their king and their God, and look upward.

## bestir → move — II Samuel 5:24
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Move" is a genuinely different, common word attested independently by Geneva1599 ("remoue") and YLT ("move sharply") for the same call to action.
- NEW: And let it be, when thou hearest the sound of a going in the tops of the mulberry trees, that then thou shalt move thyself: for then shall the Lord go out before thee, to smite the host of the Philistines.

## bethink → take it to their hearts — I Kings 8:47
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "return themselves in the land" is ambiguous and does not convey inward reflection. RWebster's and Darby's "take it to their hearts" attest a genuinely different, clearer construction for the same act of inward repentance.
- NEW: Yet if they shall take it to their hearts in the land whither they were carried captives, and repent, and make supplication unto thee in the land of them that carried them captives, saying, We have sinned, and have done perversely, we have committed wickedness;

## bethink → take it to their hearts — II Chronicles 6:37
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Consistent with I Kings 8:47 above; RWebster and Darby both attest the same construction here.
- NEW: Yet if they take it to their hearts in the land whither they are carried captive, and turn and pray unto thee in the land of their captivity, saying, We have sinned, we have done amiss, and have dealt wickedly;

## bewray → discover — Isaiah 16:3
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Discover" carries its period sense of "reveal, betray" (not the modern "find"), matches RWebster's, Webster's, and Darby's own rendering, and is common in the KJV corpus.
- NEW: Take counsel, execute judgment; make thy shadow as the night in the midst of the noonday; hide the outcasts; discover not him that wandereth.

## bier → coffin — II Samuel 3:31
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "bed" wrongly substitutes ordinary sleeping furniture for a funeral litter, which is confusing in a mourning context. Tyndale's and Geneva1599's "coffin" (used at Luke 7:14 for the identical funeral-bier structure) is a genuinely different, period-authentic word for the object carried in a funeral procession.
- NEW: And David said to Joab, and to all the people that were with him, Rend your clothes, and gird you with sackcloth, and mourn before Abner. And king David himself followed the coffin.

## bier → coffin — Luke 7:14
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Consistent with II Samuel 3:31 above; Tyndale, Geneva1599, and ACV all render this "coffin" directly.
- NEW: And he came and touched the coffin: and they that bare him stood still. And he said, Young man, I say unto thee, Arise.

## billows → floods — Psalms 42:7
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "heaps" does not fit the imagery of waves passing over the psalmist. Geneva1599's "all thy waues and thy floods are gone ouer me" attests a genuinely different, very common KJV word (Exodus 15:8's own "floods stood upright as an heap" shows the same watery sense) for the same overwhelming imagery.
- NEW: Deep calleth unto deep at the noise of thy waterspouts: all thy waves and thy floods are gone over me.

## billows → floods — Jonah 2:3
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Consistent with Psalms 42:7 above; "floods" is used here for the same reason, preserving the parallel with "waves."
- NEW: For thou hadst cast me into the deep, in the midst of the seas; and the floods compassed me about: all thy floods and thy waves passed over me.

## bishoprick → oversight — Acts 1:20
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Oversight" is a genuinely different, common word that matches YLT's own rendering of this verse exactly.
- NEW: For it is written in the book of Psalms, Let his habitation be desolate, and let no man dwell therein: and his oversight let another take.

## bitten → stung — Numbers 21:8
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "every one that is bite" is ungrammatical. "Stung" is a genuinely different, common word for the same venomous serpent-attack described throughout this passage, and preserves correct past-participle grammar.
- NEW: And the Lord said unto Moses, Make thee a fiery serpent, and set it upon a pole: and it shall come to pass, that every one that is stung, when he looketh upon it, shall live.

## bitten → stung — Numbers 21:9
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Consistent with Numbers 21:8 above; CUR's "had bite any man" is likewise ungrammatical, and "stung" resolves it correctly.
- NEW: And Moses made a serpent of brass, and put it upon a pole, and it came to pass, that if a serpent had stung any man, when he beheld the serpent of brass, he lived.

## blacker → darker — Lamentations 4:8
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "dark than a coal" is ungrammatical, dropping the required comparative. YLT's and Darby's "darker than blackness"/"darker than a coal" attest "darker," a genuinely different lemma (dark, not black) that correctly carries the comparative form.
- NEW: Their visage is darker than a coal; they are not known in the streets: their skin cleaveth to their bones; it is withered, it is become like a stick.

## blackish → turbid — Job 6:16
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "black" is a forbidden mere re-inflection of the same lemma (dropping the "-ish" qualifier). Darby attests "turbid," a genuinely different word describing water clouded and murky with melting ice, fitting the sense of streams darkened by ice-melt.
- NEW: Which are turbid by reason of the ice, and wherein the snow is hid:

## blains → sores — Exodus 9:9
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: RWebster and ACV both attest "sores," a genuinely different, extremely common KJV word (Job 2:7, Luke 16:20-21) for the same skin affliction named by the specialized medical term "blains."
- NEW: And it shall become small dust in all the land of Egypt, and shall be a boil breaking forth with sores upon man, and upon beast, throughout all the land of Egypt.

## blains → sores — Exodus 9:10
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: Consistent with Exodus 9:9 above; RWebster and ACV both attest "sores" here as well.
- NEW: And they took ashes of the furnace, and stood before Pharaoh; and Moses sprinkled it up toward heaven; and it became a boil breaking forth with sores upon man, and upon beast.

## blasphemer → railer — I Timothy 1:13
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's bare "a railing" is not a valid noun for a person and breaks the grammar of "was before a railing." "Railer" is the correctly formed noun for the same root CUR reached for, is a genuinely different word from "blasphemer," and is independently attested as a KJV-family term for one who reviles (compare "or a railer, or a drunkard," 1 Corinthians 5:11).
- NEW: Who was before a railer, and a persecutor, and injurious: but I obtained mercy, because I did it ignorantly in unbelief.
