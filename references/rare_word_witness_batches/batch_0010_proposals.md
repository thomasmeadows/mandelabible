# Rare word witness batch 0010 — proposals (entries 361–400)

## blaze → spread — Mark 1:45
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Spread" is a genuinely different, common word that matches YLT's, Darby's, and ASV's own rendering of this verse.
- NEW: But he went out, and began to publish it much, and to spread abroad the matter, insomuch that Jesus could no more openly enter into the city, but was without in desert places: and they came to him from every quarter.

## bleating → crying — I Samuel 15:14
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "speaking of the sheep" wrongly implies sheep speak. "Crying" is a genuinely different, common KJV word for an animal's distress-noise, and parallels "the lowing of the oxen" that follows in the same clause.
- NEW: And Samuel said, What meaneth then this crying of the sheep in mine ears, and the lowing of the oxen which I hear?

## bleatings → pipings — Judges 5:16
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "hiss of the flocks" is wrong (a hiss is not a sheep's sound). ASV and ACV both attest "pipings for the flocks" — a different but genuine translation tradition referring to shepherds' pipe-music rather than the animals' own noise — supplying a genuinely different, coherent word.
- NEW: Why abodest thou among the sheepfolds, to hear the pipings of the flocks? For the divisions of Reuben there were great searchings of heart.

## blindfolded → covered — Luke 22:64
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Covered" is a genuinely different, common word that matches Darby's and ACV's own rendering ("covering him up" / "having covered him") of this verse.
- NEW: And when they had covered him, they struck him on the face, and asked him, saying, Prophesy, who is it that smote thee?

## bloodguiltiness → blood — Psalms 51:14
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Blood" is a genuinely different, common word that matches Geneva1599's and YLT's own rendering of this verse exactly.
- NEW: Deliver me from blood, O God, thou God of my salvation: and my tongue shall sing aloud of thy righteousness.

## bloodthirsty → men of blood — Proverbs 29:10
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "The man hate the upright" has a broken subject-verb agreement and drops the essential descriptor. YLT's "Men of blood hate the perfect" attests a genuinely different, grammatical construction naming the same violent character.
- NEW: Men of blood hate the upright: but the just seek his soul.

## bloomed → produced — Numbers 17:8
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "shewed blossoms" is an unusual and unclear phrasing. ASV's and ACV's "produced blossoms" attest a genuinely different, common word that reads naturally alongside "brought forth buds."
- NEW: And it came to pass, that on the morrow Moses went into the tabernacle of witness; and, behold, the rod of Aaron for the house of Levi was budded, and brought forth buds, and produced blossoms, and yielded almonds.

## blueness → stripes that wound — Proverbs 20:30
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "The wounds of a wound" is a broken duplication. ASV's "Stripes that wound cleanse away evil" attests a genuinely different construction that resolves the nonsense while preserving the sense of corrective punishment.
- NEW: Stripes that wound cleanse away evil: so do stripes the inward parts of the belly.

## blunt → dull — Ecclesiastes 10:10
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "the iron be set" loses the sense entirely (a dull edge requiring more strength). BSB independently attests "dull," a genuinely different, common English word for the same lack of a sharp edge.
- NEW: If the iron be dull, and he do not whet the edge, then must he put to more strength: but wisdom is profitable to direct.

## bolled → in bloom — Exodus 9:31
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "Bolled" is a specialized botanical term for flax in seed-pod stage; ASV and ACV both attest "in bloom," a genuinely different, common phrase for the same growth stage.
- NEW: And the flax and the barley was smitten: for the barley was in the ear, and the flax was in bloom.

## bolt → lock — II Samuel 13:17
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "shod the door" is nonsensical (to shoe, not to fasten). Geneva1599 attests "locke the doore," a genuinely different, common word for securing a door.
- NEW: Then he called his servant that ministered unto him, and said, Put now this woman out from me, and lock the door after her.

## bolted → locked — II Samuel 13:18
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "inclosed the door" is an unusual, unclear use of "inclosed" for shutting a door. Consistent with the II Samuel 13:17 ruling above, Geneva1599's "locked the doore" supplies the same genuinely different word.
- NEW: And she had a garment of divers colours upon her: for with such robes were the king's daughters that were virgins apparelled. Then his servant brought her out, and locked the door after her.

## bondmaid → bondwoman — Leviticus 19:20
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "handmaid" weakens the legal point of the verse, which turns on the woman's unfree status ("because she was not free") — a handmaid in KJV usage need not be unfree. Darby attests "bondwoman," a genuinely different word that keeps the essential bond/unfree sense the verse's ruling depends on.
- NEW: And whosoever lieth carnally with a woman, that is a bondwoman, betrothed to an husband, and not at all redeemed, nor freedom given her; she shall be scourged; they shall not be put to death, because she was not free.
