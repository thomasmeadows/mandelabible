## cogitations → thoughts — Daniel 7:28

- rationale: "thoughts" is a genuinely different word from "cogitations," is grammatically clean, and matches Wycliffe "thouytis" and DRC "thoughts" directly.
- NEW: Hitherto is the end of the matter. As for me Daniel, my thoughts much troubled me, and my countenance changed in me: but I kept the matter in my heart.

## collar → neck — Job 30:18
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "mouth of my coat" is a modern-witness borrowing (YLT, not period) that reads as a mixed metaphor. "Neck" is a genuinely different, common word naming the same part of a garment, and matches the sense of Geneva/Wycliffe/DRC's "collar" (the coat's neck-opening) as well as BBE's modern gloss "neck of my coat."
- NEW: By the great force of my disease is my garment changed: it bindeth me about as the neck of my coat.

## collars → jewels — Judges 8:26
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR duplicates "chains" later in the same verse ("...chains, and purple raiment... beside the chains..."), which is clumsy. Geneva1599 ("collers and iewels") and DRC ("ornaments, and jewels") both independently attest "jewels" for this item, giving a period-supported word that avoids the repetition.
- NEW: And the weight of the golden earrings that he requested was a thousand and seven hundred shekels of gold; beside ornaments, and jewels, and purple raiment that was on the kings of Midian, and beside the chains that were about their camels' necks.

## college → second quarter — II Kings 22:14
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's bare "the second" is grammatically incomplete. Wycliffe's "the secounde dwellyng" and DRC's "the Second" attest a place-reference sense for the word "college" (a district of Jerusalem); "quarter" is added for clarity per the not-one-for-one rule, matching the sense-completing pattern also seen in Darby/ASV.
- NEW: So Hilkiah the priest, and Ahikam, and Achbor, and Shaphan, and Asahiah, went unto Huldah the prophetess, the wife of Shallum the son of Tikvah, the son of Harhas, keeper of the wardrobe; (now she dwelt in Jerusalem in the second quarter;) and they communed with her.

## collops → fat (rephrased) — Job 15:27

## comers → them that draw nigh — Hebrews 10:1
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "the worshippers thereunto" is grammatically awkward ("worshippers thereunto" doesn't parse naturally). Wycliffe's "men neiyinge" (men drawing nigh) attests the underlying sense of "comers," and this clause-level rewrite mirrors that construction while replacing "comers" outright, consistent with rule 1 (not one-for-one).
- NEW: For the law having a shadow of good things to come, and not the very image of the things, can never with those sacrifices which they offered year by year continually make them that draw nigh thereunto perfect.

## comfortable → pleasant — II Samuel 14:17
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's "shall now be rest" is ungrammatical (a noun standing where an adjective is needed). "Pleasant" is a genuinely different, common adjective conveying the same sense of an agreeable word, matching the "pleasant/agreeable" register of Early Modern usage without the grammar break.
- NEW: Then thine handmaid said, The word of my lord the king shall now be pleasant: for as an angel of God, so is my lord the king to discern good and bad: therefore the Lord thy God will be with thee.

## comfortedst → hast eased — Isaiah 12:1
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR merely stripped the -edst ending from the same word ("thou comfort me"), which both breaks thou/verb agreement and violates the rule against re-inflecting rather than replacing the flagged word. DRC's "hast comforted me" and Darby's "hast comforted me" model the have + past-participle construction; substituting a genuinely different verb, "eased" (a plain, common Early Modern word for relief from distress), keeps the grammar correct.
- NEW: And in that day thou shalt say, O Lord, I will praise thee: though thou wast angry with me, thine anger is turned away, and thou hast eased me.

## commissions → decrees — Ezra 8:36
