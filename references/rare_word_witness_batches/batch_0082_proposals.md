# Rare word witness batch 0082 — proposals

## respite → space — I Samuel 11:3
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "feeble" is a nonsense substitution unrelated to meaning. "space" is common in the KJV corpus for a granted span of time (27 hits, e.g. Rev 2:21 "space to repent") and matches the sense of Geneva's "respet"/DRC "Allow us seven days".
- NEW: And the elders of Jabesh said unto him, Give us seven days' space, that we may send messengers unto all the coasts of Israel: and then, if there be no man to save us, we will come out to thee.

## restingplace → resting place — Jeremiah 50:6
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: The single concatenated "restingplace" is not standard KJV orthography; Geneva1599, DRC, Webster, ASV, UKJV, and ACV all render it as the two-word phrase "resting place" (3 KJV hits), which is the corpus's customary form.
- NEW: My people hath been lost sheep: their shepherds have caused them to go astray, they have turned them away on the mountains: they have gone from mountain to hill, they have forgotten their resting place.

## restorer → comforter — Ruth 4:15
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "return" is a bare noun that cannot govern "of thy life" sensibly. Wycliffe/DRC read "comforte thy soul"; "comforter" is well-attested in the KJV corpus (7 hits, e.g. Job, Lamentations) and preserves the sense of one who restores wellbeing, paired naturally with "nourisher".
- NEW: And he shall be unto thee a comforter of thy life, and a nourisher of thine old age: for thy daughter in law, which loveth thee, which is better to thee than seven sons, hath born him.

## restorer → maker — Isaiah 58:12
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "return" cannot take "of paths to dwell in" as its object. No witness offers a different agent-noun (all keep "restorer"/"restoring"). "maker" (25 KJV hits) supplies a common, period-authentic noun that preserves the sense of one who fashions the paths anew for habitation, without duplicating "repairer" already in the verse.
- NEW: And they that shall be of thee shall build the old waste places: thou shalt raise up the foundations of many generations; and thou shalt be called, The repairer of the breach, The maker of paths to dwell in.

## restrain → withdrawest — Job 15:8
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "gird" is nonsensical with "wisdom to thyself." YLT reads "withdrawest thou unto thee wisdom" for this verse; "withdraw" is attested in the KJV corpus (19 hits) and the exact inflected form "withdrawest" occurs once (Job 33:17), so it is not a novel coinage.
- NEW: Hast thou heard the secret of God? and dost thou withdraw wisdom to thyself?

## restrain → gird upon thee — Psalms 76:10
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "gird" alone is transitive in a way the KJV does not use it here (one girds a thing "upon" oneself, not girds the thing itself). ASV/Darby read "shalt thou gird upon thee" for this verse; "gird" is well-attested (94 hits) and adding "upon thee" completes the authentic construction.
- NEW: Surely the wrath of man shall praise thee: the remainder of wrath shalt thou gird upon thee.

## restrainest → takest away — Job 15:4
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "makest" inverts the meaning (making prayer, rather than hindering it). "hinderest"/"withholdest" return zero hits in the KJV corpus, so both are barred by rule 4. Wycliffe/DRC read "hast taken away prayers before God"; "takest away" is attested in the corpus (1 hit) and "takest" itself is common (9 hits), giving a genuinely different, corpus-supported phrase.
- NEW: Yea, thou castest off fear, and takest away prayer before God.

## restraint → let — I Samuel 14:6
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "let" (hindrance) is a genuinely different word from "restraint", is period-authentic English (the legal idiom "without let or hindrance"), and CUR's grammar is sound as written; no witness offers a superior alternative to this already King-James-agent-reviewed choice.
- NEW: And Jonathan said to the young man that bare his armour, Come, and let us go over unto the garrison of these uncircumcised: it may be that the Lord will work for us: for there is no let to the Lord to save by many or by few.

## retire → withdraw — II Samuel 11:15
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "return" is ambiguous (return to where?). Darby reads "withdraw from him" for this verse; "withdraw" is attested in the KJV corpus (19 hits) and gives the clear sense of retreating from Uriah's side.
- NEW: And he wrote in the letter, saying, Set ye Uriah in the forefront of the hottest battle, and withdraw ye from him, that he may be smitten, and die.

## retire → flee — Jeremiah 4:6
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "return" contradicts the context (the people are told to seek refuge in Zion, not go back). ASV reads "flee for safety, stay not" and Geneva "prepare to flee, stay not"; "flee" is extremely common in the KJV corpus (123 hits) and fits the sense of taking refuge from the coming destruction.
- NEW: Set up the standard toward Zion: flee, stay not: for I will bring evil from the north, and a great destruction.

## retired → turned — Judges 20:39
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "turn" is in the wrong tense (present, amid a past-tense narrative — "began to smite" is past). ASV reads "turned in the battle" for this verse; "turned" is extremely common (490 hits) and restores tense agreement with the surrounding narrative.
- NEW: And when the men of Israel turned in the battle, Benjamin began to smite and kill of the men of Israel about thirty persons: for they said, Surely they are smitten down before us, as in the first battle.

## retired → scattered — II Samuel 20:22
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "scattered" is a genuinely different word from "retired", matches YLT/Darby/ASV ("scattered"/"dispersed") for this verse, is common in the KJV corpus (74 hits), and CUR's grammar and meaning are already sound.
- NEW: Then the woman went unto all the people in her wisdom. And they cut off the head of Sheba the son of Bichri, and cast it out to Joab. And he blew a trumpet, and they scattered from the city, every man to his tent. And Joab returned to Jerusalem unto the king.

## revealer → interpreter — Daniel 2:47
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "reveal" is the wrong part of speech (a bare verb cannot follow the article "a"). No witness supplies a common alternative noun ("unveiler," "reveiler" are unattested/rare). Daniel's own context repeatedly turns on "interpretation" of mysteries (Dan 5:12, "an interpreter of dreams"); "interpreter" is attested in the corpus (4 hits) and fits the thematic register of Daniel 2 without merely re-inflecting the flagged word.
- NEW: The king answered unto Daniel, and said, Of a truth it is, that your God is a God of gods, and a Lord of kings, and an interpreter of secrets, seeing thou couldest reveal this secret.

## revellings → banquetings — Galatians 5:21
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "banquetings" is a genuinely different, period-authentic word — it is the KJV's own rendering at I Peter 4:3 for this very concept — and is attested in the corpus; CUR's grammar and sense are sound.
- NEW: Envyings, murders, drunkenness, banquetings, and such like: of the which I tell you before, as I have also told you in time past, that they which do such things shall not inherit the kingdom of God.

## revellings → rioting — I Peter 4:3
- verdict: REVISE

- NEW: For the time past of our life may suffice us to have wrought the will of the Gentiles, when we walked in lasciviousness, lusts, excess of wine, rioting, banquetings, and abominable idolatries:

## reverend → fearful — Psalms 111:9
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "afraid" inverts the sense (the name itself cannot be afraid). Geneva1599/YLT read "holy and fearefull/fearful is his Name" for this verse; "fearful" in its archaic sense of "awe-inspiring" is attested in the corpus (15 hits, e.g. Exodus 15:11 "fearful in praises").
- NEW: He sent redemption unto his people: he hath commanded his covenant for ever: holy and fearful is his name.

## revile → speak evil of — Exodus 22:28
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR "curse" duplicates the word "curse" already used later in the same verse ("nor curse the ruler"), which is forbidden. Geneva1599 reads "neither speake euil of the ruler," and "speak evil of" is an established KJV phrase (10 hits, e.g. Titus 3:2) giving a genuinely different rendering for the first clause.
- NEW: Thou shalt not speak evil of the gods, nor curse the ruler of thy people.

## revile → reproach — Matthew 5:11
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: While CUR "curse" is not ungrammatical, ASV renders this exact verse "when men shall reproach you," and "reproach" is far better attested in the KJV corpus (115 hits) than "curse" would need to be here, giving a stronger period-authentic fit.
- NEW: Blessed are ye, when men shall reproach you, and persecute you, and shall say all manner of evil against you falsely, for my sake.

## revilings → rebukes — Zephaniah 2:8
