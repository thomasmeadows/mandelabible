## slanderers → accusers — I Timothy 3:11
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "accusers" is attested in KJV (9 verses, cf. YLT "false accusers") and is a genuinely different word fitting the sense of malicious speech.
- NEW: Even so must their wives be grave, not accusers, sober, faithful in all things.

## slanderously → falsely — Romans 3:8
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "falsely" matches BBE and is common in KJV (23 verses), fitting the sense of a false report.
- NEW: And not rather, (as we be falsely reported, and as some affirm that we say,) Let us do evil, that good may come? whose damnation is just.

## slang → sling — I Samuel 17:49
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "sling" is the wrong tense (present-tense verb dropped into a past-tense narrative: "took...and sling it and smote" is grammatically broken). "slung" does not occur in the KJV corpus, so "cast" (extremely common, 497 verses) is used instead, matching the sense of hurling the stone and preserving correct tense throughout.
- NEW: And David put his hand in his bag, and took thence a stone, and cast it, and smote the Philistine in his forehead, that the stone sunk into his forehead; and he fell upon his face to the earth.

- NEW: Is Israel a servant? is he a homeborn bondman? why is he spoiled?

## sleeper → sleep — Jonah 1:6
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR turns the noun into the wrong part of speech ("O sleep" as vocative address is ungrammatical). Rephrasing as a verb clause avoids reintroducing "sleeper" while using the well-attested verb "sleepest" (4 verses).
- NEW: So the shipmaster came to him, and said unto him, What meanest thou, that thou sleepest? arise, call upon thy God, if so be that God will think upon us, that we perish not.

## sleight → subtlety — Ephesians 4:14
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "subtlety" does not occur anywhere in the KJV corpus (0 occurrences), failing the "must not be KJV-rare" rule. "guile" is attested (11 verses, e.g. 1 Peter 2:1) and is a genuinely different word fitting the sense of crafty deception, and does not duplicate "cunning craftiness" already present in the verse.
- NEW: That we henceforth be no more children, tossed to and fro, and carried about with every wind of doctrine, by the guile of men, and cunning craftiness, whereby they lie in wait to deceive;

## slothfulness → idleness — Proverbs 19:15
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR ("Idleness casteth...") duplicates the root of "idle soul" later in the same verse. "sluggard" is attested (6 verses, e.g. Proverbs 6:6, 6:9) and, rephrased as the subject of the clause, avoids the duplication while preserving the Hebrew's personifying sense of laziness.
- NEW: The sluggard falleth into a deep sleep; and an idle soul shall suffer hunger.

## slothfulness → slackness — Ecclesiastes 10:18
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: this is a circular rare-for-rare swap (slothfulness: 2 occurrences; slackness: 1 occurrence — each independently flagged elsewhere in this project). Rephrasing with "slack" as an adjective describing the hands avoids both the rare noun and duplicating "idleness" in the same verse, and mirrors the Hebrew's own synonymous parallelism (Wycliffe/DRC: "feebleness of hands").
- NEW: Because the hands are slack the building decayeth; and through idleness of the hands the house droppeth through.

## slumberings → dreams — Job 33:15
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR duplicates "a dream," which already opens the same verse. Rephrasing the final clause as "as they rest upon the bed" avoids reusing any word already present (dream, vision, sleep) while preserving the sense of repose.
- NEW: In a dream, in a vision of the night, when deep sleep falleth upon men, as they rest upon the bed;

## smart → evil — Proverbs 11:15
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR is broken grammar ("shall evil for it" is not a verb phrase). "suffer" is common in KJV and fits DRC's own rendering ("shall be afflicted with evil").
- NEW: He that is surety for a stranger shall suffer for it: and he that hateth suretiship is sure.

## smiters → murderers — Isaiah 50:6
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "murderers" overstates the violence — the servant continues speaking afterward, describing ongoing shame and spitting, not death; "murderers" implies a fatal outcome inconsistent with the narrative. Rephrasing as a relative clause with the attested verb "beat" (common in KJV) preserves the sense of a severe but non-fatal beating without merely re-inflecting "smiters."
- NEW: I gave my back to them that beat me, and my cheeks to them that plucked off the hair: I hid not my face from shame and spitting.
