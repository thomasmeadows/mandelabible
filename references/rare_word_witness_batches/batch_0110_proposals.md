# Batch 0110 proposals

## wateredst → didst water — Deuteronomy 11:10
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: CUR's bare "water" breaks the second-person past-tense parallel with "sowedst." Webster's periphrastic "didst sow... and water" shows this construction is acceptable; here restoring "didst water" keeps the clause unambiguously past-tense and second-person.
- NEW: For the land, whither thou goest in to possess it, is not as the land of Egypt, from whence ye came out, where thou sowedst thy seed, and didst water it with thy foot, as a garden of herbs:

## wavereth → doubteth — James 1:6

- NEW: But let him ask in faith, nothing doubting. For he that doubteth is like a wave of the sea driven with the wind and tossed.

- NEW: And when he came, lo, Eli sat upon a seat by the way watching: for his heart trembled for the ark of God. And when the man came into the city, and told it, all the city cried out.

## weaken → didst lay low — Isaiah 14:12

- NEW: How art thou fallen from heaven, O Lucifer, son of the morning! how art thou cut down to the ground, which didst lay low the nations!

## weakeneth → looseth — Job 12:21
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "draweth the strength" is a meaning error (draweth ≠ weakens). ASV renders "looseth the belt of the strong"; "looseth" alone (keeping "the strength of the mighty," which is not flagged) is a common KJV verb that correctly conveys weakening.
- NEW: He poureth contempt upon princes, and looseth the strength of the mighty.

## weakeneth → maketh feeble — Jeremiah 38:4

- NEW: Therefore the princes said unto the king, We beseech thee, let this man be put to death: for thus he maketh feeble the hands of the men of war that remain in this city, and the hands of all the people, in speaking such words unto them: for this man seeketh not the welfare of this people, but the hurt.

## weave → make — Isaiah 19:9
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "they that like networks" is nonsensical. CUR's problem word aside, using "work" (as Geneva/RWebster suggest) would duplicate "work" already used earlier in this verse ("they that work in fine flax"); "make" is common, distinct, and preserves the sense.
- NEW: Moreover they that work in fine flax, and they that make networks, shall be confounded.

## weave → spin — Isaiah 59:5
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "and like the spider's web" is nonsensical. All witnesses use "weave" (the flagged word itself), so a genuinely different verb is needed; "spin" is the common, plain English verb specifically associated with spiders' webs.
- NEW: They hatch cockatrice’ eggs, and spin the spider’s web: he that eateth of their eggs dieth, and that which is crushed breaketh out into a viper.

## weaver → webster — Exodus 35:35
- verdict: REVISE
- rationale: "of the like, even of them that do any work" drops the craftsman category entirely. "webster" is the genuine period English word for a weaver (root of the surname); it names the craft without duplicating "work," which already saturates this verse.
- NEW: Them hath he filled with wisdom of heart, to work all manner of work, of the engraver, and of the cunning workman, and of the embroiderer, in blue, and in purple, in scarlet, and in fine linen, and of the webster, even of them that do any work, and of those that devise cunning work.

## weaver → webster — Isaiah 38:12
- verdict: REVISE

- NEW: Mine age is departed, and is removed from me as a shepherd’s tent: I have cut off like a webster my life: he will cut me off with pining sickness: from day even to night wilt thou make an end of me.
