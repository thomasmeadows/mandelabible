# Batch 0009 proposals — groups 321–337

## wantonness → self will — Genesis 49:6
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Wantonness" occurs only here in the restored corpus. Every witness bar Darby/ACV/ASV/BBE/BSB reads "self-will"/"selfe will" at this clause (Wycliffe "her wille", Tyndale/Geneva/DRC/Webster/RWebster all "self will"). "Self" (6) and "will" (3837) are both healthy, common KJV words, and "self will" is itself an attested KJV phrase elsewhere (e.g. 2 Peter 2:10 "selfwilled").
- NEW: O my soul, come not thou into their secret; unto their assembly, mine honour, be not thou joined: for in their anger they slew a man, and in their self will they digged down a wall.

## wardrobe → robes — II Kings 10:22
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Wardrobe" is a hapax here; Geneva/ACV/ASV/UKJV/RWebster/Webster/Darby render the office as "vestry"/"vestments," but "vestry" and "vestments" are both wholly unattested (0) in the restored corpus, so they would only trade one rare word for two. "Robes" (11) is healthy and matches BBE/BSB's rendering of the same office and object.
- NEW: And he said unto him that was over the robes, Bring forth garments for all the worshippers of Baal. And he brought them forth garments.

## watercourse → conduit — II Samuel 5:8
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Watercourse" appears only here. "Gutter," the majority-witness reading (Webster/RWebster/YLT), is wholly unattested (0) in the restored corpus. "Conduit" (4) is close to but below the ≥5 guideline, yet it is the standing, authentic 1611 KJV word for a water channel (2 Kings 18:17, 20:20; Isaiah 7:3, 36:2), so it is the best available common substitute rather than introducing an unattested word.
- NEW: And David said on that day, Whosoever getteth up to the conduit, and smiteth the Jebusites, and the lame and the blind, that are hated of David's soul, he shall be chief and captain. Wherefore they said, The blind and the lame shall not come into the house.

## wearisome → burdensome — Acts 24:4
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Wearisome" is a hapax. Nearly every witness reads "tedious," but "tedious" is wholly unattested (0) in the restored corpus. "Burdensome" (5) meets the healthy-count threshold, is itself a genuine KJV word (Nehemiah 5:18; 2 Corinthians 12:14, "I will not be burdensome to you"), and carries the same sense of imposing upon another's patience.
- NEW: Notwithstanding, that I be not further burdensome unto thee, I pray thee that thou wouldest hear us of thy gentleness a few words.

## webster → workman — Exodus 35:35
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Webster" (an archaic term for a female weaver) occurs twice in the corpus. "Weaver," the near-unanimous witness reading, is wholly unattested (0) — swapping one rare word for another equally rare word would not solve the deviation. "Workman" (14) is healthy, already used earlier in this very verse ("the cunning workman"), and names the same class of craftsman performing the textile work described.
- NEW: Them hath he filled with wisdom of heart, to work all manner of work, of the engraver, and of the cunning workman, and of the workman in needlework, in blue, and in purple, in scarlet, and in fine linen, and of the workman, even of them that do any work, and of those that devise cunning work.

## webster → workman — Isaiah 38:12
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: Same group as Exodus 35:35. "Weaver" remains unattested (0) despite being the majority witness reading, so "workman" (14) is used again for consistency and because it is the healthiest attested common noun for a craftsman in this corpus. This softens the weaving-specific image ("cut off like a weaver" evokes thread from a loom), which is noted as a real loss of nuance, but no attested common word preserves the loom metaphor.
- NEW: Mine age is departed, and is removed from me as a shepherd's tent: I have cut off like a workman my life: he will cut me off with pining sickness: from day even to night wilt thou make an end of me.

## whatever → wherein — II Corinthians 11:21
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Whatever" occurs only here. Geneva, Webster, Darby, and ASV all render this clause with "wherein," which is very well attested (167) in the restored corpus and produces the idiomatic KJV construction "wherein any man is bold."
- NEW: I speak as concerning reproach, as though we had been weak. Howbeit wherein any is bold, I speak foolishly, I am bold also.

- NEW: Yea, whereunto might the strength of their hands profit me, in whom old age was perished?

## whiteness → (no safe swap) — Jeremiah 30:6
- verdict: NO-SAFE-SWAP
- rationale: "Whiteness" is a hapax. The overwhelming majority witness reading, "paleness," is wholly unattested (0) in the restored corpus, and no other attested common word names this specific pallor of fear/labor without distorting the sense — "green" (43, BBE only) and "yellow" (6, Wycliffe/DRC) shift the image to a wholly different color, and bare "pale" is itself only attested once. Best failed candidate: "paleness" (0 occurrences, would itself remain a rare word).
- NEW: (no change — see rationale)

## wink → twinkling — I Corinthians 15:52
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Wink" appears once. This is the world-famous KJV idiom "in the twinkling of an eye," attested nearly unanimously across every witness (Wycliffe, Tyndale, Geneva, DRC, Webster, RWebster, YLT, Darby, ASV, UKJV, BSB) at this exact verse, and it does not recur elsewhere in scripture even in the authentic 1611 text — it is inherently a hapax phrase, not an artifact of corruption. This is a correction of the underlying reading rather than a search for a "common" alternative, akin to fixing a misspelling.
- NEW: In a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last horn: for the trumpet shall sound, and the dead shall be raised incorruptible, and we shall be changed.

## winnowing → fan — Matthew 3:12
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Winnowing fork" is a hapax phrase here. "Fan" (7) is healthy and is the actual traditional Authorized Version word at this verse ("whose fan is in his hand"), matching Tyndale, Geneva, DRC, Webster, RWebster, UKJV.
- NEW: Whose fan is in His hand, and He will throughly purge His threshing floor, and gather His wheat into the barn; but He will burn up the chaff with fire that never shall be quenched.

## witch → (no safe swap) — Isaiah 57:3
- verdict: NO-SAFE-SWAP
- rationale: "Witch" is a hapax. The near-unanimous witness reading "sorceress" is itself only attested once (1) elsewhere in the restored corpus, below the healthy-count threshold, and no stronger common candidate preserves the sense without collision — "harlot" (40) is well attested but would duplicate the following clause's "whore" and shift the referent from sorcery to prostitution; "adulteress" (5) likewise reassigns the referent to match the wrong half of the parallel. Best failed candidate: "sorceress" (only 1 occurrence, still rare).
- NEW: (no change — see rationale)

"Sorcerer" matches the reading found in Webster, RWebster, and (in cognate form) DRC/Geneva "soothsayer."
- NEW: Balaam also the son of Beor, the sorcerer, did the children of Israel slay with the sword among them that were slain by them.

## woodsman → (rephrased with "hew") — Isaiah 14:8
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Woodsman" is a hapax. The witness readings split between "hewer"/"feller," both wholly unattested (0) as nouns in the restored corpus. Rather than trade one rare noun for another, the verb "hew" (12, healthy) is used in a common phrase, "no man is come up to hew us down," preserving the same image of a woodcutter felling the cedar with common, well-attested words ("man," 2622; "hew," 12).
- NEW: Yea, the fir trees rejoice at thee, and the cedars of Lebanon, saying, Since thou art laid down, no man is come up to hew us down.

## worthily → well — Proverbs 31:29
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Worthily" is a hapax. The majority witness reading "virtuously" is wholly unattested (0) in the restored corpus. "Well" (257) is extremely well attested, plain, and carries the identical sense of praiseworthy conduct ("have done well").
- NEW: Many daughters have done well, but thou excellest them all.

## wouldst → wouldest — John 11:40
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: This is a spelling-standardization fix, not a meaning change: "wouldst" is a rarer spelling variant of the same second-person-singular form, attested only once, while "wouldest" is the base text's own standard spelling, attested 37 times (including Webster/RWebster at this very verse). Correcting to the corpus's normal orthography removes the deviation without altering the archaic grammar (thou + wouldest is authentic KJV usage).
- NEW: Jesus saith unto her, Said I not unto thee, that, if thou wouldest believe, thou shouldst see the glory of God?

## wrists → arms — Ezekiel 13:18
- verdict: REPLACE
- rationale: "Wrists" is a hapax. Neither witness alternative ("arm-holes," "elbows") is attested in the corpus (0 each). "Arms" (29) is healthy and is close in sense to Webster/RWebster's "arm-holes" and Darby's own "wrists," naming the same body part to which the magic bands/pillows are said to be sewn.
- NEW: And say, Thus saith the Lord God; Woe to the women that join pillows to all arms, and make vails upon the head of every stature to hunt souls! Will ye hunt the souls of my people, and will ye save the souls alive that come unto you?
